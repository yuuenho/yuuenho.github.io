While attendance for the Royal Ontario Museum have generally gone up over the last 10 years, the number of students that has attended has consistently been lower than the provincial average in Ontario during that time. In 2016, approximately 110,000 students attended the Royal Ontario Museum, which only makes up 5% of students between the age of 5 to 18, across Ontario. Click here for more info.

Scope

While the ROM currently runs programming under the brand ROM Kids, it’s currently limited to on-site activities and content. For my final project at Bitmaker, I wanted to extend this experience so that kids could engage with ROM programming remotely.
‍
In 2012, the ROM launched an app called ScopifyROM, which presented users supplementary content while they explored the ROM. Like the current ROMKids programming, the app required users to be physically present in the ROM, which fails to attract new attendees who aren't already committed to going to the ROM already.
‍
This project was not affiliated with the Royal Ontario Museum and spanned just over three weeks working alone to conduct research, ideate possible solutions, develop the UI, build the branding and create all visual artifacts. The result was an interactive age-appropriate high fidelity prototype with video content.

Research

This was my first time building something for kids so before I started conducting my one-on-one interviews, I spent some time researching the nuances in designing for kids. Thankfully there’s no shortage of literature on the topic. These are some of the resources I used before getting started.

UX Collective
inVision
Sesame Street

In determining my audience, I selected and interviewed kids between the age of 6 to 8 and their parents. My audience primarily lived in the GTA but outside the City of Toronto. I chose this specific age group since they had more of an ability to orally communicate their likes and dislikes in a richer context. I started my research with family members that had kids and had them introduce me to their friends with kids to build a bigger group to interview.

My research informed me that young kids possess very little familiarity with museums - most could knew about fossils or statutes but couldn't attribute to being in a museum.
Kids who had been to the museum had difficulty recalling what was there or what they had seen unless prompted by their parent.
The range of apps kids enjoyed varied greatly in app category and topic, but enjoyment oriented.

Additionally I interviewed parents as well as they would be the individuals who were concerned with the degree in which their children consumed digital media.

Parents had a preference for their kids to use apps or technology for learning rather than just for fun.
While parents often considered the ROM as a worthwhile outing, places like the zoo or an aquarium would win as kids were familiar with them.

Design

Referencing the research I looked in the differences between designing for kids and for adult audience, reading comprehension and dexterity are important factors when building an application for children. Typically, the educational software industry will segment the child market into distinct age brackets of 2-3 years in range to accommodate for rapid changes in mental and physical ability. This was also the reason I set my research age bracket to that 6 - 8 age range. These factors went into how I set out my initiate ideas and designs in terms of spacing, layout, interactions and content.
‍
Early stage designs such as sketches and paper prototypes made it difficult for user testing among kids. They struggled with conceptualizing and anticipating responses from the paper prototype when presented with block wireframes with textual or oral descriptions of what would happen. I ended up doing some simple mock drawings with toy representations of museum objects and guiding kids roughly through storytelling to lead them through each activity.

While having experienced success with InVision in the past, the limited transition animations made it less appealing for a hi-fidelity prototype in this particular project. I decided to experiment with an alternative tool, Flinto which provided enhanced transitions and allowed for easier mapping of  flows and error states. This solution proved to be less distracting for the test group. While those were all great reasons to use Flinto the biggest benefit from using Flinto was that it allowed for audio feedback which was really important for users that needed more guidance throughout the app and those with low reading comprehension.

Ideation

An animated guide in the form of a cartoon dinosaur joins users to help them navigate through different activities. The guide serves as a familiar face and allows for non-intrusive prompts and reminders. The guide also delivers the on-boarding experience as a conversation between the character and the user. Outside of the digital product of this app, characters within ROMKids could lead to different products to grow the brand as well.

A plush version was made for the ROMKids app demo presentation.

The app is narrated by one of the character guides and allows for children to have an enjoyable experience even if their reading comprehension is at a lower level. Narration is also useful for children who struggle with dyslexia by providing an alternative medium for content. Using voice prompts as well as reminders, it also help kids who experience difficulty focusing.

All content is laid out in video lessons with options for narration and captioning. Videos are presented in a short infographic style and may be accessed and rewatched at any time. By allowing users to pair what they hear with what they read, the app presents an opportunity for the user improve their reading comprehension.

After each module, users are prompted to test their knowledge via a short quiz. Users are encouraged with positive reinforcement and have the ability to make several attempts at the correct answer. Both correct and incorrect responses result in prompts with additional information about the correct answer.

The game is designed to allow free exploration; kids have the ability to revisit activities and jump  and skip through content. While this is to capture the experience of the ROM, it also allows kids who are young to pick and choose which areas they want to spend time in. This is a common design philosophy that game developers such as Toca Boca and Sago Mini take when designing their kids apps.

Gamification elements are applied in order to encourage users to collect artifacts. Completion of activities and quizzes reward users with items from the ROM, each one provides added educational content about the ROM. While kids generally like collecting badges or stickers even on a virtual platform, the content was specifically selected from the ROM so that kids can pair that experience when they do go to the ROM.

Visually all the designs were done using flat design so that they could be created quickly while still being able to portray the objects that they represented.

The types of interactions were kept minimal. Recognizing that younger users have reduced manual dexterity, the primary interaction used was one finger tap. In future iterations swipe and drag would be also suitable. It was important for this project not to focus too much on the content but instead the way in which content was presented and interacts with users through the platform.

What's next

One thing that really stood out for me for this project was that as I did more research and went back to validate my ideas, the more I found that I was not creating just a digital project but creating a brand for individuals to connect with the Royal Ontario Museum especially for a younger audience.
‍
One thing that I initially missed was defining my KPI for parents. While I had set my focus on kids for measuring my success. Overall even if kids enjoyed the app, parents were the ones who eventually would decide whether or not they would visit the museum. While I included elements around education which were what parents were concerned about, in my testing I didn't go back to test and validate if the app would be something the parents would approve of and met their needs. With this in mind, it's important for me to recognize that when considering users, I needs to consider all users - even the indirect ones.

Barkbuds

Meet dogs near you.
Scope
Many dogs like playing with other dogs but sometimes their humans are a bit concerned with who they are playing with. Will the dogs get along? Will one of the dogs be aggressive? What if they have fleas?
‍
I wanted to to give dog owners greater control over meeting and socializing their furbabies with other dogs nearby. I created Barkbuds as a mobile app to allow users to narrow down others dogs they may see by building a profile and connecting with dogs that match the profile desired.

This project spanned just over a week working alone to conduct research, ideate possible solutions, develop the UI, build the branding and create all visual artifacts. The result was an interactive prototype.
While there are a lot of apps and services out there for dog sitting or walkers such Rover or Bark n Borrow or apps tailored to dating while with a dog, there are very few options for users to casually introduce their dogs to other dogs in a controlled environment. Some shelters and community groups would organize larger meetups for dog owners but can often be overly stimulating for some dogs and take a lot of control away from dog owners to select who they would want their dogs interacting with.
As the goal was to create a friendly and intuitive social networking platform, I turned to traditional dating apps to model our problem space. I selected to model the platform on OKCupid, which had a comprehensive on-boarding process to better define a suitable match while having an inviting tone and doesn't overload users with too many questions all at once.
Competitive Analysis
Ideation
When interviewing users, temperament came up consistently as a concern for users when hoping to pair up their dogs. A challenge that arose in the research was that while most users agreed temperament was important to consider, there was no clear definition between users of what that meant. Some attributed temperament as a behaviour while others defined it as moods. Doing some addition online research into canine psychology still left me with a lot of questions
‍
Rather than having temperament described in one dimension, I took the most common behaviours which users identified as important for them and allowed users to select on a spectrum the closest to what they believe described their dog. This seems appropriate for users as going into moods may be good for owners when understanding their own dogs but wasn't too helpful when looking at socializing dogs with other dogs.
Iterations
My iteration process started with lo-fi wireframes, taking into consideration early user stories and flows. Earlier versions were modified after user testing was done.
A large focus of this project was designing an appropriate and user friendly on-boarding process that didn’t feel like too much work to get setup and start looking at profiles. With conventional dating apps, users typically only need to two make a profile for themselves and highlight what they're looking for in a match.
‍
Users builds three profiles: the user’s, their dog, and the prospective matching dog. The initial on-boarding focuses on the user and their dog. Additional prompts are spaced out throughout use of the app to continue building the profile. Giving users more control in this process, they are able to skip and complete these parts later.

Learnings
While this project is unlikely to continue through product development, it was a really fun introduction to several new tools that I now work with quite frequently for hi-fi prototyping including inVision and Principle. This project was an exciting way for me to be introduced to animation and motion.

The biggest insight I could take from this project is that while the needs and concerns of users can often be the same, users can perceive and approach that problem-space in radically different ways. In recognizing something like temperament as a complex idea, it allows me to think of my users in a richer context and design more appropriately.

About me and my experiences...

Born and raised in Toronto - my style has always mirrored the city's vibrancy and quirkiness. Growing up in Toronto and starting my career in accessibility working with students with disabilities. I've spent my journey trying to make the world better for others and in doing so trying to understand their experiences. From that, I've learned that each experience - whether from culture or disability - can contribute to any endeavour as a perspective of diversity and makes whatever you produce that much richer when included.

When I'm not designing webpages and crafting thoughtful user experiences, I'm designing costumes. I really enjoy exploring different ways to make costumes using things like LEDs and Arduino boards. If you're curious, you can check them out here.
