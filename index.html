This project was to bridge the experience so that kids didn’t have to be at the ROM physically to be able to engage with ROM programming. ROM Kids was envisioned to provide kids with digital access to the ROM in a age-appropriate format through mobile app.
Being a part of a big family means lots of kids, what's surprising is that while figuring out a weekend activity with my nieces and nephew that most of them haven't been to the Royal Ontario Museum, let alone was aware of what they could see or do there. While the Royal Ontario Museum's (ROM) annual attendance has increased over the last 10 years, numbers of kids who go each year has declined by half during that time.
When talking to parents, distance and time were definitely factors for parents but for many of which they relied on their kids to let them know if going to the ROM it would be something they the kids wanted to do and would be excited for.
This was especially problematic in cases where the kids weren’t even aware of what they would expect if they went to the ROM or how that experience would look like in comparison to things like the zoo which generally kids have a greater understanding and expectation of.
Some helpful resources I went to while designing for kids.

Initial Insights
- For kids, different ages will attribute greatly to the development of a child's ability (ie. reading comprehension or dexterity
- Younger kids prefer tablets while as age increases the preference shifts to laptops and desktops
Given the time frame I had with only 3 weeks, I opted not to build a platform that tried to hit every age group but focus in on a particular range. As my challenge was to create the experience of the ROM in a digital space, I wanted my target audience to be younger so that they would have limited prior experiences with other museums or the ROM specifically. I chose kids between 6 - 8 since they had more of an ability to orally communicate their likes and dislikes in a richer context.
Parents said that they often didn’t have enough time to do everything that they wanted to do with their kids. As much as parents typically thought the ROM was a worthwhile outting for their family, it was not one many had done or prioritized relative to other city attractions. Often opting for other attractions that their kids asked to go to such as the zoo or aquarium.
Some parents with multiple children struggled with finding attractions that were engaging to all their kids as age differed.
In terms of their kids and technology, most parents were concerned with the degree in digital consumption their kids was participating in. However, they felt apps for educational purposes were significantly less of a concern for them when it came to how their kids spent their time.
The ROM had actually launched an app in 2012 called ScopifyROM which showed additional content while you explored the ROM. The challenge with both is that they rely on users to already be at the ROM and do not market to those who haven’t decided if they are interested in going.
Understanding that for rich feedback from user testing among children, much of the visual content needed to be available early on. Block wireframes and description of content were things that even from hallway testing with kids were not effective. All the design artifacts were rapidly produced but users were not part of the early processes. Much of the design choices for the interface were modelled off various games and apps that were currently popular for kids, this was something I chose not to focus too much time on in order to prioritize a working prototype.

In the past I’ve worked with Invision and while it’s terrific for quick prototypes, the limitations of the transitions made it less appealing for this particular project. Flinto was a tool that had been brought to my attention a few times in the weeks leading up to this project, so I decided to take the bullet and dive into it. Flinto while still glitchy and temperamental combined prototyping with animation.
In addition to the transitions, Flinto allowed for easier mapping of wireflows and error states for kids that didn’t feel disruptive. If kids tried accessing something they couldn’t instead of being taken to a screen that was not what they were expecting, they would simply be informed to select something else on the screen. This was done through looped wireflows where the transition triggers an audio prompt by a character guide.
A character guide joins users to help them navigate through the different activities. They act as a familiar face and allows for non-intrusive prompts or reminders. They also allow for any of the on-boarding portions as a conversation between the character and the user rather than filling in forms.


The app is narrated (often by the character guide) this allows for kids to have enjoyable experience even if their reading comprehension is at a lower level. The narration also also for kids who struggle with dyslexia to have an alternative medium for the content and the prompts and reminders help for kids who struggle with focusing. Narration of this app is brought courtesy of some friends of mine :)
Activities are connected to the video lessons for users to complete. They are interactive and apply the content learned for the activity in the prototype users are shown different items from the video and users gain a better understanding of the content relative to those items. Kids have the ability to rewatch or skip content as well.
All the content is laid out in video lessons with the options of narration with or without captioning. The option allows for kids to increase their reading comprehension by pairing what they hear with something they can read along to.

Flat design was chosen as the primary esthetic as it allowed for all the illustrations to be produced quite quickly with minimal detail while not taking away the quality of the illustrations especially as there were often multiple objects in any given frame. They added a cartoon element in which kids enjoyed and were more engaged with.
Customization was an added feature allowing kids to dress up the characters, this added a level of enjoyment for users. This was also a way for kids who had siblings to distinguish their profiles if they shared devices. While thinking about future versions, this may be a possible avenue for clients like the ROM to approach premium payment models with added skins in addition to access of premium content and the activities.
A part in which was a big struggle for me was definitely keeping this in the scope of a UX project specifically as the project was really quite big. My initial plan was actually to build a visual design language that would be able to be applied for different institutions rather than focus on one specifically and their content. While the three weeks was not a long enough of a time frame for that. It is something that played into many of my decisions and hopefully something that would still apply to the changes of other institutions who are in similar situations to the ROM like the Art Gallery of Ontario.
Largely my two measures of success and performance were basically if kids would want to continue playing with the app and if this would lead to them asking their parents to go to the ROM. I think a part that I would have liked to break down more is whether or not elements of the platform and the interactions were the reasons for those success or if it was just the content. I think most of this relied on needing more time to develop stronger theories and then user testing to gather findings.
As this project was spanned only across 3 weeks, a lots of things were rushed through to ensure the prototype was ready. Continuing to develop the app, I would love to revisit and explore different things such as the copy and the audio from the app, to make it more age-appropriate and inviting. Additionally I would love to see how different activities could be used in the app as well that engages and educates as well.
‍
Things like consideration for baby gating would also be another, as the initial prototype didn’t require any payment methods or associations with emails or other websites, no baby gates were set up. This would need to be revisited should those elements be introduced especially in the case of a premium models.
While this project is not made with the intent to launch, things I would love to explore and potentially how this could be bridge with the ROM - this would include things as AR exhibits in their Samuel European Galleries which showcase history rooms and decor. This would be an excellent opportunity to show what life would be like in that era in the context of those rooms.
Other things that took a back seat because of time was the development for access between iPad and iPhone devices. It was initially designed for iPad but important to note that it’s easier for kids to hold and explore the ROM with an iPhone for future features like AR.


ROM Kids is a brand and this app was intended as a a product under the brand.

Barkbuds

Meet dogs near you.
Scope
Many dogs like playing with other dogs but sometimes their humans are a bit concerned with who they are playing with. Will the dogs get along? Will one of the dogs be aggressive? What if they have fleas?
‍
I wanted to to give dog owners greater control over meeting and socializing their furbabies with other dogs nearby. I created Barkbuds as a mobile app to allow users to narrow down others dogs they may see by building a profile and connecting with dogs that match the profile desired.

This project spanned just over a week working alone to conduct research, ideate possible solutions, develop the UI, build the branding and create all visual artifacts. The result was an interactive prototype.
While there are a lot of apps and services out there for dog sitting or walkers such Rover or Bark n Borrow or apps tailored to dating while with a dog, there are very few options for users to casually introduce their dogs to other dogs in a controlled environment. Some shelters and community groups would organize larger meetups for dog owners but can often be overly stimulating for some dogs and take a lot of control away from dog owners to select who they would want their dogs interacting with.
As the goal was to create a friendly and intuitive social networking platform, I turned to traditional dating apps to model our problem space. I selected to model the platform on OKCupid, which had a comprehensive on-boarding process to better define a suitable match while having an inviting tone and doesn't overload users with too many questions all at once.
Competitive Analysis
Ideation
When interviewing users, temperament came up consistently as a concern for users when hoping to pair up their dogs. A challenge that arose in the research was that while most users agreed temperament was important to consider, there was no clear definition between users of what that meant. Some attributed temperament as a behaviour while others defined it as moods. Doing some addition online research into canine psychology still left me with a lot of questions
‍
Rather than having temperament described in one dimension, I took the most common behaviours which users identified as important for them and allowed users to select on a spectrum the closest to what they believe described their dog. This seems appropriate for users as going into moods may be good for owners when understanding their own dogs but wasn't too helpful when looking at socializing dogs with other dogs.
Iterations
My iteration process started with lo-fi wireframes, taking into consideration early user stories and flows. Earlier versions were modified after user testing was done.
A large focus of this project was designing an appropriate and user friendly on-boarding process that didn’t feel like too much work to get setup and start looking at profiles. With conventional dating apps, users typically only need to two make a profile for themselves and highlight what they're looking for in a match.
‍
Users builds three profiles: the user’s, their dog, and the prospective matching dog. The initial on-boarding focuses on the user and their dog. Additional prompts are spaced out throughout use of the app to continue building the profile. Giving users more control in this process, they are able to skip and complete these parts later.

My higher fidelity iterations included varying designs and colours, which eventually were refined based off of AB testing, user feedback and accessibility tools such as contrast analyzer.
Animations for the prototype were designed in Principle. Creating a variety of motion to either familiarize users with specific interactions or to provide intuitive feedback to the user's interactions.
Onboarding slides mirrored user interactions to form habits.
Autocomplete prompts users to select instead of long scrolling.
Matches produce an immediate CTA or option to revisit action.

Learnings
While this project is unlikely to continue through product development, it was a really fun introduction to several new tools that I now work with quite frequently for hi-fi prototyping including inVision and Principle. This project was an exciting way for me to be introduced to animation and motion.

The biggest insight I could take from this project is that while the needs and concerns of users can often be the same, users can perceive and approach that problem-space in radically different ways. In recognizing something like temperament as a complex idea, it allows me to think of my users in a richer context and design more appropriately.

About me and my experiences...

Born and raised in Toronto - my style has always mirrored the city's vibrancy and quirkiness. Growing up in Toronto and starting my career in accessibility working with students with disabilities. I've spent my journey trying to make the world better for others and in doing so trying to understand their experiences. From that, I've learned that each experience - whether from culture or disability - can contribute to any endeavour as a perspective of diversity and makes whatever you produce that much richer when included.

When I'm not designing webpages and crafting thoughtful user experiences, I'm designing costumes. I really enjoy exploring different ways to make costumes using things like LEDs and Arduino boards. If you're curious, you can check them out here.
